HIT: The Third Case



About HIT: The Third Case

HIT: The Third Case, the third installment in the HIT franchise, received mixed to positive reviews upon its release on May 1, 2025, with a general consensus that Nani's performance is the film's strongest asset, though the movie's excessive violence and uneven storytelling are significant drawbacks. The film, directed by Sailesh Kolanu and starring Nani as the brooding and intense police officer Arjun Sarkar, marks a shift from the character-driven investigative thrillers of its predecessors toward a more stylized, action-heavy narrative. While some critics praise its gritty tone and high-octane sequences, others argue it sacrifices the franchise's original intent of compelling mystery for over-the-top action and gratuitous gore.

Nani's performance is widely lauded as the film's highlight, with reviewers describing it as his most intense and stylized outing yet, effectively anchoring the film through its chaotic narrative. His portrayal of a traumatized, rage-fueled cop is noted for its emotional depth and commanding presence, with standout moments in both action and silent, introspective scenes. Srinidhi Shetty, in her Telugu debut, is acknowledged for holding her own, though her romantic subplot is seen as a narrative distraction by some. The supporting cast, including Prateik Babbar as the antagonist and Samuthirakani as Nani’s father, receive mixed feedback, with the villain’s characterization criticized for lacking menace and depth.

The film’s technical aspects are a point of divergence. Sanu Varghese’s cinematography is praised for capturing diverse landscapes, and the pre-climax action sequences are highlighted as well-executed and thrilling. However, the editing is criticized for being uneven, particularly in the second half, and the background score by Mickey J Meyer is described as a mixed bag—effective in the second half but lacking impact in the first, with some calling it generic and unsuitable for the film’s tone. The use of AI and CGI to depict crime scenes and gore is noted for its realism, which may unsettle sensitive viewers.

A major point of contention is the film’s excessive violence and graphic content, which some reviewers feel is gratuitous and not adequately justified by the story. The film’s A certificate, while allowing broader access, has raised concerns about its suitability for younger audiences. The narrative’s reliance on tropes from global hits like Squid Game and the overuse of cuss words are also criticized as attempts to appear edgy without substance. The film’s pacing is uneven, with the first half being taut and engaging, but the second half described as chaotic and overly drawn out.
